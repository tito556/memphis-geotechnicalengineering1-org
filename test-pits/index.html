Professional Exploratory Test Pit Services in Memphis, Tennessee

Technical studies that support your project.

One of the most persistent mistakes we see on Memphis construction sites is the assumption that the subsurface is uniform. A geotechnical report based solely on desk studies or a single boring can miss buried debris, old stream channels, or the dramatic variation in loess thickness that characterizes the Bluff City. An exploratory test pit exposes the actual strata in plan and section, allowing the engineer to observe bedding, fractures, moisture, and the interface between the natural loess and the underlying alluvium. In a city built atop four distinct terraces of the Mississippi River, from the high bluffs of downtown to the flood-prone bottomlands near the Wolf River, skipping direct visual inspection is a gamble no prudent developer should take.

A single test pit in Memphis loess exposes more about site conditions than three borings alone—visible stratification, moisture, and cut-slope behavior all at once.

Scope of work

The geology beneath Memphis tells a story of wind and water. The city sits on a thick mantle of loess—wind-deposited silt—that can range from 15 to over 70 feet deep as you move east from the Mississippi River toward the Shelby County line. Beneath the loess lies the Jackson Formation, a stiff, sandy clay, and deeper still, the Memphis Sand aquifer that supplies the region with some of the purest drinking water in the country. An exploratory test pit lets us examine this loess directly: we log its color, consistency, moisture content, and any calcareous nodules or root casts that signal paleosol horizons. Where the loess thins near the Wolf and Nonconnah Creek floodplains, the pits often reveal interbedded silts and sands that demand a different approach to footing design and drainage. We conduct field vane shear tests at multiple depths, collect bulk and undisturbed samples for laboratory classification, and photograph every lift so the project team sees exactly what we saw.

Area-specific notes

Located on the Fourth Chickasaw Bluff, Memphis has an elevation of about 337 feet above sea level, yet the terrain descends abruptly into floodplains where the water table may be less than 5 feet below ground surface. Furthermore, the city is situated in the New Madrid Seismic Zone, one of the most seismically active areas east of the Rockies; the USGS indicates that Shelby County faces a significant probabilistic ground-motion hazard. In this context, shallow test pits are essential because they uncover the depth and continuity of the loess cap, which directly affects site classification according to ASCE 7-22. If a site has thin loess overlying loose alluvium, it could be classified as Site Class E or even F, resulting in increased seismic design demands. Additionally, we use these pits to evaluate collapsible soil behavior—a known issue with dry loess—and to examine cut-slope stability when projects include basements or retaining walls along the bluff.

Standards used

Relevant standards for this work include ASTM D2488 for soil description and identification via visual-manual procedures, OSHA 29 CFR 1926 Subpart P for excavation safety (covering shoring, benching, and sloping), and IBC 2024 Section 1803 which governs geotechnical investigations including test pit observation and sampling requirements.

Top questions

What depth can an exploratory test pit reach in Memphis soils?

In the loess uplands, we typically use a standard rubber-tire backhoe to excavate to depths of 12–14 feet. For deeper explorations or sites with a high water table near the Wolf River bottoms, a track-mounted excavator is employed, capable of reaching 18–20 feet when proper benching is applied. Generally, the limiting factors are groundwater inflow or OSHA safety regulations rather than the equipment's capabilities.

How much does an exploratory test pit investigation cost in the Memphis area?

A standard single-pit investigation that includes full logging, field testing, and sampling costs between US$540 and US$720. The exact price varies based on depth, excavator access, the number of pits, and whether undisturbed Shelby tube samples are needed for lab analysis.

How long after excavation can a test pit remain open?

The pit remains open only for logging, photography, and sampling—usually 2 to 4 hours. Memphis loess can hold vertical cuts for short durations but is prone to slaking during rain. For pits exceeding 5 feet in depth, OSHA mandates sloping, benching, or a protective system, and we backfill the pit as soon as the investigation ends.

Can you collect undisturbed samples from a test pit for laboratory testing?

Certainly. We collect block samples by manually trimming a pedestal of undisturbed loess and wrapping it in paraffin and cheesecloth, or by horizontally pushing thin-walled Shelby tubes from the pit wall. These samples undergo testing in our accredited lab for consolidation, shear strength, and collapse potential.
